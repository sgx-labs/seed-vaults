# Salary Negotiation Tactics

## The Core Idea

Negotiation is expected. Hiring managers budget for it. Failing to negotiate is leaving money on the table — typically $5,000-$15,000+ over the initial offer. The key is being prepared, professional, and specific.

## The Playbook

### Step 1: Deflect Early Salary Questions
If asked about salary expectations before receiving an offer:
- "I'd prefer to learn more about the role before discussing compensation. What is the budgeted range for this position?"
- "I'm flexible on compensation — I'm more focused on finding the right fit. Can you share the range?"

### Step 2: Receive the Offer with Enthusiasm
When you get the offer:
- Express genuine excitement about the role
- Thank them
- Ask for the complete offer in writing
- Ask for time to review ("I'd like to take a day or two to review everything carefully")

### Step 3: Research Your Counter
- Check Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Payscale, and LinkedIn Salary for the role + location
- Talk to people in similar roles
- Factor in your unique qualifications (specialized skills, certifications, impact)

### Step 4: Make Your Counter
- Be specific: "Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for $X" (not a range)
- Justify it: "Given my [X years of experience in Y] and my track record of [accomplishment], I believe $X reflects the value I would bring"
- Be professional and warm — this is a collaboration, not a confrontation

### Step 5: If They Cannot Meet Your Number
Negotiate other items:
- Signing bonus (often easier to approve than base salary increases)
- Extra PTO days
- Remote work days
- Professional development budget
- Earlier performance review (with salary adjustment potential)
- Title upgrade
- Start date flexibility

## Rules
- **Never accept on the spot.** Always ask for time to review.
- **Never lie about competing offers.** It can backfire catastrophically.
- **Never give an ultimatum.** Keep the tone collaborative.
- **Get the final offer in writing.** Verbal commitments are not binding.
