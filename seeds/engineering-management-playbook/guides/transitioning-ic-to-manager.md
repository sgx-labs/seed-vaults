# Transitioning from IC to Manager

## The Identity Shift

The hardest part of becoming a manager isn't learning processes -- it's the identity shift. You've spent years building an identity around technical excellence. Now your value comes from other people's output. This feels wrong at first. It gets easier.

## What Changes

### Your Definition of "Productive" Changes
- **Before**: "I shipped a feature today."
- **After**: "I unblocked three people, had a difficult feedback conversation, and hired someone great."

It's harder to measure and less immediately satisfying. But your impact is multiplied across every person you manage.

### Your Calendar Changes
- **Before**: Long blocks of focus time, occasional meetings
- **After**: Meetings, 1-on-1s, context-switching, and stolen 30-minute blocks for your own work

This is not a failure. This IS the work.

### Your Relationship with Code Changes
- **Before**: You wrote the best code on the team
- **After**: You review code, but you don't write production code regularly

Don't fight this. The moment you start coding critical-path features, you're neglecting your team AND creating a dependency on yourself.

## Month 1: The Basics

### Do:
- Set up 1-on-1s with every direct report (see `guides/running-first-one-on-one.md`)
- Listen more than you talk. Your first month is for learning, not changing.
- Ask your manager: "What does success look like for me in 90 days?"
- Find a management mentor (another EM, your skip-level, external coach)

### Don't:
- Reorganize anything. You don't have enough context yet.
- Stop all coding abruptly. Taper off over 2-3 months.
- Try to be everyone's friend. You're their manager now. The relationship changed.
- Compare yourself to experienced managers. You're a beginner. That's okay.

## Month 2-3: Finding Your Style

### Delegation
Start delegating the technical work you used to own. It's painful. Someone will do it differently (maybe worse). That's the cost of developing your team.

### Feedback
Start giving feedback regularly. Small, frequent, specific. Don't save it up for a quarterly review. See `entities/feedback-frameworks.md`.

### Difficult Conversations
You'll need to have at least one conversation you've been avoiding. Someone's code quality, someone's attitude in meetings, someone's missed deadlines. Do it sooner rather than later. See `guides/giving-difficult-feedback.md`.

## Common Traps

### The Player-Coach Trap
You keep doing IC work "because nobody else can." This is usually ego, not reality. Delegate, develop, or hire.

### The Approval Trap
You review and approve every decision. Your team stops making decisions without you. Step back. Let them own outcomes.

### The People-Pleaser Trap
You avoid hard conversations because you want to be liked. Management requires being respected more than being liked. You can be both, but when they conflict, choose respected.

### The Reverse Trap
Three months in, you decide "management isn't for me." Sometimes this is true. But sometimes it's just the discomfort of being a beginner again. Give it 6-12 months before deciding.

## Deciding If It's Right for You

After 6-12 months, ask yourself:
- Do I get energy from helping others succeed?
- Can I tolerate ambiguity and delayed gratification?
- Am I okay with my impact being invisible (no one says "great management in that sprint")?
- Can I have uncomfortable conversations regularly?

If the answers are mostly no, going back to IC is not a failure. It's self-awareness.
