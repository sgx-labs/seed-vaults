# Giving Difficult Feedback

## Why This Is Hard

Difficult feedback is hard because you care. If you didn't care about the person or the outcome, you'd either say nothing or say it bluntly. The challenge is being honest AND kind. Not one or the other -- both.

## Before the Conversation

### Check Your Motivation
Ask yourself: "Am I giving this feedback to help them, or to make myself feel better?" If it's the latter (venting frustration, asserting authority), wait until you can approach it constructively.

### Prepare Specifics
Vague feedback is worse than no feedback. "You need to communicate better" is useless. "In the last two design reviews, you presented your solution without acknowledging the constraints others raised, which shut down discussion" is actionable.

Prepare:
- **Specific situation(s)**: When and where
- **Observable behavior**: What you saw or heard (not your interpretation)
- **Impact**: The effect on the team, project, or individual
- **What you'd like to see instead**: The alternative behavior

### Choose the Setting
- Always private. Never in a meeting, Slack channel, or with others present.
- In person or video if possible. Text-based feedback for sensitive topics is too easily misread.
- Enough time (30 minutes minimum). Don't squeeze it in before another meeting.

## The Conversation

### Opening (Don't Bury the Lead)

Don't start with small talk and then ambush them. Signal that this is a feedback conversation:

"I want to share some feedback about [topic]. It's important to me because I care about your growth and the team. Can we talk about it now?"

### Deliver the Feedback (2-3 minutes)

Use the SBI framework (Situation, Behavior, Impact):

"In last Tuesday's sprint planning [situation], you pushed back on the timeline estimate without asking the team for their reasoning [behavior]. Two engineers told me afterward they felt dismissed, and one stopped contributing for the rest of the meeting [impact]."

Then pause. Let them respond. Don't fill the silence.

### Listen (This Is the Hard Part)

They might:
- **Agree**: "You're right, I was frustrated and didn't handle it well." Great. Move to next steps.
- **Disagree**: "That's not how I saw it." Ask: "Help me understand your perspective." Maybe you're missing context.
- **Get defensive**: "I only pushed back because the estimate was wrong." Acknowledge: "I hear you on the estimate. The feedback is about how you raised it, not whether the concern was valid."
- **Get emotional**: "I feel like I can't do anything right." Empathize: "That's not what I'm saying. You do a lot of things really well. This is one area where I think you can improve."

### Agree on Next Steps (5 minutes)

Don't end with just the feedback. End with a path forward:

- "What do you think you could do differently next time?"
- "How can I support you on this?"
- "Let's check in on this in our next 1-on-1."

Write the action items in your shared 1-on-1 doc.

## After the Conversation

- **Follow up**: In your next 1-on-1, reference it. "I noticed you asked the team about their reasoning before pushing back on the timeline this week. That's exactly the change I was hoping to see."
- **Don't hold a grudge**: If they improve, the feedback worked. Don't bring it up again.
- **Don't avoid them**: After a hard conversation, some managers unconsciously avoid the person. Don't. Stay connected.

## When NOT to Give Feedback

- **When you're angry**: Wait until you're calm. Feedback from anger is punishment, not coaching.
- **In public**: Never. Even "constructive" public feedback humiliates.
- **About things they can't change**: Personality, communication style that works for them, personal preferences. Focus on behaviors and outcomes.
- **Third-hand feedback**: "Someone told me you did X." Unless you can share the source, this is gossip. Either verify firsthand or don't raise it.
