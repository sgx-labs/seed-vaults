# Managing Through Organizational Change

## Types of Change

Organizational change isn't monolithic. Different types require different management responses:

- **Reorgs**: Teams merge, split, or move under new leadership
- **Layoffs**: Headcount reduction -- the most traumatic
- **Leadership changes**: New VP, new CTO, new direction
- **Strategy pivots**: Product direction shifts, priorities reorder
- **Acquisitions**: See `research/ma-engineering-integration.md`
- **Process changes**: New tools, methodologies, or workflows

## Your Role: Shield and Translator

During organizational change, you're the interface between leadership decisions and your team's daily reality. You have two jobs:

### Shield
Protect your team from chaos. Filter the rumor mill. Reduce uncertainty where you can. They should be able to focus on their work while you handle the politics.

### Translate
Convert leadership messaging (often vague, optimistic, and jargon-heavy) into honest, specific, actionable information for your team.

**Leadership says**: "We're streamlining our organization to better align with our strategic priorities."
**You translate**: "Two teams are merging. Here's what changes for us, what doesn't, and what I don't know yet."

## The Communication Framework for Change

### What to Share
- **Facts you know**: Concrete changes with dates
- **What doesn't change**: People need anchors
- **What you don't know**: "I don't have the answer to that yet" is better than silence or false reassurance
- **The timeline**: When will decisions be made? When will things stabilize?

### How to Share
- **Team meeting for announcements**: Everyone hears the same thing at the same time
- **1-on-1s for individual concerns**: "How does this affect me?" deserves a private conversation
- **Written follow-up**: People absorb bad news poorly in real-time. Send a summary they can re-read

### What NOT to Do
- **Don't lie**: "Nobody's losing their job" when you don't know that
- **Don't speculate**: "I think the VP is on her way out" -- even if true, this creates panic
- **Don't disappear**: Going quiet during change signals that things are worse than people think
- **Don't pretend you agree if you don't**: Your team will sense the dissonance. You can say "I pushed back on this decision but ultimately I'm supporting it because [reason]"

## During Layoffs

The hardest type of change. If you're asked to lay people off:

**For those leaving:**
- Be direct, compassionate, brief (see `hub/pip-and-separation.md`)
- Offer to be a reference
- Help with job search if you can (introductions, recommendations)

**For those staying (survivor guilt is real):**
- Acknowledge the loss: "This is hard. We lost good people."
- Address workload: "Here's how we're redistributing work. We're not just adding their work to your plates."
- Rebuild trust: "I'll tell you what I know as soon as I know it."
- Watch for burnout: survivors often work harder out of fear

**For yourself:**
- Process your own emotions. You can't support your team if you're falling apart.
- Talk to a peer, mentor, or coach. Not your team.

## When NOT to Apply This Framework

- **Don't over-manage routine changes**: A new sprint tool doesn't need a change management strategy. Don't create anxiety about changes that aren't anxiety-worthy.
- **Don't shield your team from reality**: There's a difference between protecting them from chaos and hiding important information. They're adults. Trust them with the truth.
- **Don't absorb all the stress yourself**: You're a human, not a buffer. Share the emotional load with peer managers or your own manager.

## In Reality

You'll be told things "in confidence" that affect your team and asked not to share them. This is the hardest part of management. You can't tell your team about the reorg until it's announced, but they can tell something's off. Do your best: "There are some changes being discussed. I'll share details as soon as I can. For now, the best thing we can do is keep delivering."
