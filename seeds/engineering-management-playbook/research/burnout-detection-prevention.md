# Burnout Detection and Prevention

## Understanding Burnout

Burnout isn't "being tired." It's a chronic state of exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced effectiveness caused by sustained workplace stress. The WHO classifies it as an occupational phenomenon with three dimensions:

1. **Exhaustion**: Physical and emotional depletion. "I can't do this anymore."
2. **Cynicism**: Detachment from work, colleagues, and purpose. "None of this matters."
3. **Reduced efficacy**: Feeling incompetent despite evidence of competence. "I'm not good enough."

Engineers are particularly vulnerable because of on-call demands, constant context-switching, imposter syndrome culture, and the myth that passionate people should love working long hours.

## Early Warning Signs

### Behavioral Changes (Observable)
- Output declining without a clear reason
- Withdrawing from conversations, retros, social interactions
- Increased irritability or cynicism in discussions
- Missing meetings or showing up late (new pattern)
- Working odd hours (compensating for unproductive "normal" hours)
- Asking about PTO balance or leave policies (testing exit options)

### Self-Reported Signals (In 1-on-1s)
- "I'm fine" with no elaboration (deflection)
- "I just need to push through this sprint" (every sprint)
- Loss of interest in topics they used to be passionate about
- Physical symptoms: headaches, insomnia, difficulty concentrating
- "I don't care which approach we take" from someone who used to have strong opinions

### Systemic Indicators
- On-call pages increasing without corresponding system growth
- Unplanned work exceeding 30% of capacity for multiple sprints
- Team consistently working evenings/weekends
- No one taking PTO for extended periods

## Prevention (Before Burnout Starts)

### Sustainable Workload
- **Aim for 80% capacity in sprint planning**: Leave 20% for unplanned work, learning, and breathing room.
- **Protect weekends**: If someone works a weekend, give them a day off. Make this policy, not a favor.
- **Rotate on-call fairly**: No one should be on-call more than 1 week in 4. Compensate on-call with time off.
- **Limit WIP**: No engineer should have more than 2 items in progress simultaneously.

### Purposeful Work
- **Connect work to impact**: "This feature will reduce checkout failures by 20%" not "this is ticket PROJ-4521."
- **Eliminate toil**: If someone spends >20% of their time on repetitive manual work, automate it or stop doing it.
- **Growth opportunities**: People burn out faster when they feel stagnant. Mix maintenance with new work.

### Recovery Culture
- **Model rest**: Take PTO yourself. Leave at reasonable hours. Don't Slack on weekends.
- **Celebrate recovery**: "Alex took a week off and came back with a great idea for the caching problem." Not: "Alex was gone and we had to cover."
- **No hero worship**: "They worked all weekend to save the launch" should be followed by "and we're fixing the process so nobody has to do that again."

## Intervention (When Burnout Is Happening)

1. **Name it**: "I'm worried you might be burning out. I'm seeing [specific observations]. Am I reading this right?"
2. **Listen without fixing**: Let them talk. Don't jump to solutions.
3. **Reduce load immediately**: Remove non-essential work. Cancel unnecessary meetings. Extend deadlines.
4. **Suggest resources**: EAP, therapy, coaching. Frame it as strength: "The best athletes have coaches."
5. **Follow up**: Check in weekly. Recovery isn't instant.
6. **Address root causes**: If the burnout is systemic (too much on-call, too few people, unrealistic deadlines), fix the system. Individual coping can't compensate for broken systems.

## When NOT to Apply This Framework

- **Don't diagnose**: You're a manager, not a therapist. If someone is in crisis, connect them with professionals.
- **Don't force vulnerability**: "You seem burned out" in a team meeting is not helpful. This is a private conversation.
- **Don't assume the cause**: Burnout might be from work, from outside work, or both. Ask, don't assume.

## In Reality

You'll have a high performer who insists they're fine while showing every sign of burnout. They'll resist intervention because their identity is tied to being the reliable one. This is when you push: "I need you at your best for the long term. Right now, the best thing you can do for the team is rest."
