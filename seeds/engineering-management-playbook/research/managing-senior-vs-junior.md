# Managing Senior Engineers vs Juniors

## The Core Difference

Junior engineers need direction. Senior engineers need context. If you manage seniors like juniors, they'll feel micromanaged and leave. If you manage juniors like seniors, they'll feel abandoned and struggle. The skill is reading what each person needs and adapting.

## Managing Junior Engineers (IC1-IC2)

### What They Need
- **Clear tasks with defined scope**: "Build the user profile endpoint that returns these fields, handles these errors, and has these tests." Not "figure out the user profile stuff."
- **Frequent check-ins**: Daily or every-other-day for the first few months. Not because you distrust them -- because they often don't know what they don't know and won't ask.
- **Fast feedback loops**: Review their PRs within hours, not days. The learning happens in the review.
- **Permission to be slow**: Explicitly tell them: "I expect this to take you 3x longer than it would take a senior. That's normal. Take the time to learn."
- **A safe space to fail**: Their mistakes are small and cheap. Let them make them.

### Common Mistakes
- **Over-scoping tasks**: Giving them a "stretch project" before they've mastered the basics.
- **Insufficient context**: They can't "just figure it out" because they lack the mental models.
- **Comparing them to seniors**: "When Alex was at your level, they were already doing X" is destructive.
- **Neglecting soft skills**: Junior engineers need help with communication, estimation, and asking for help -- not just coding.

## Managing Senior Engineers (IC3-IC4)

### What They Need
- **Context, not instructions**: "Here's the business problem. Here's what we've tried. Here's the constraints. What do you think?"
- **Autonomy with accountability**: Set the destination, let them choose the route.
- **Strategic conversations**: They're bored by tactical minutiae. Engage them in system design, architecture, team direction.
- **Challenge**: If the work isn't stretching them, they'll find challenge elsewhere (usually at another company).
- **Honest feedback**: Senior engineers get less feedback because managers assume they don't need it. They do. It's just harder to give because the gaps are more nuanced.

### Common Mistakes
- **Reviewing every decision**: If you asked someone to lead a project, let them lead it. Questioning every choice is micromanagement with extra steps.
- **Using them as a crutch**: "Give it to Alex, they'll handle it" burns out your best people and stunts everyone else's growth.
- **Ignoring career development**: Senior engineers plateau without deliberate growth. "What does Staff level look like for you?" is a conversation worth having.
- **Treating them as pseudo-managers**: Not every senior engineer wants to mentor, lead meetings, or do project management. Some want to go deep. Respect that.

## Managing Staff+ Engineers (IC5+)

### What They Need
- **Organizational problems, not technical tasks**: Their impact comes from influencing direction, not writing code.
- **Air cover**: They often need to say unpopular things ("we need to rewrite this" or "this strategy won't work"). Your job: ensure they can.
- **Access to leadership**: They need to understand business context to make good technical decisions.
- **Patience**: Staff-level work is often invisible for months before it pays off. Don't judge them on sprint velocity.

## When NOT to Apply These Distinctions

- **Don't use level as a personality proxy**: Some senior engineers are introverted and need more check-ins. Some juniors are self-directed and need less hand-holding. Calibrate to the individual, not just the title.
- **Don't create a caste system**: If juniors can't challenge seniors' ideas, you've built a hierarchy, not a team.
- **Domain expertise overrides seniority**: A junior engineer who's deeply knowledgeable in a specific domain should lead in that domain, regardless of level.

## In Reality

The hardest management challenge: an underperforming senior engineer. They were promoted based on technical skill, but they've stopped growing, aren't mentoring, or are delivering at a mid-level pace. This requires the most direct, honest conversation you'll have.
