# Compensation Philosophy and Leveling

## Why This Matters

Compensation is the area where manager inaction causes the most damage. Underpaid engineers eventually discover the gap -- and they don't get angry about the money. They get angry about the disrespect. A thoughtful compensation philosophy prevents this.

## Building a Leveling Framework

### The Level Structure

Most engineering orgs use a two-track structure:

**Individual Contributor Track:**
- **IC1** (Junior): Completes well-defined tasks with guidance
- **IC2** (Mid): Independently delivers features, requires less oversight
- **IC3** (Senior): Owns significant workstreams, mentors others, makes technical decisions
- **IC4** (Staff): Drives technical direction across a team, solves ambiguous problems
- **IC5** (Senior Staff): Influences technical direction across multiple teams
- **IC6** (Principal): Shapes company-wide technical strategy
- **IC7** (Distinguished): Industry-recognized expertise, defines the state of the art

**Management Track:**
- **M1** (Engineering Manager): Manages 4-8 ICs, owns team delivery and people growth
- **M2** (Senior EM): Manages managers or a large team, owns org-level outcomes
- **M3** (Director): Owns a department, sets strategy, manages multiple teams
- **M4** (VP): Owns engineering function or a major business area
- **M5** (SVP/CTO): Owns entire engineering org

### What Each Level Means

For each level, define expectations across 4 dimensions:

1. **Scope**: Size of problems they tackle (task -> feature -> system -> org -> company)
2. **Autonomy**: How much direction they need (directed -> guided -> independent -> leading)
3. **Impact**: Where their work matters (their code -> their team -> multiple teams -> company)
4. **Influence**: How they shape others (learning -> mentoring -> leading -> defining)

Write concrete examples for each. "A Senior Engineer at our company would be expected to..." with 3-5 bullet points.

## Compensation Bands

### Structure

Each level has a band with a minimum, midpoint, and maximum:

```
IC3 (Senior): $150K ----[$175K]---- $200K
IC4 (Staff):  $180K ----[$210K]---- $240K
```

- New hires typically start at 40th-60th percentile of the band
- Progression within band happens through performance, not promotion
- Promotion moves someone into the bottom of the next band
- Bands should overlap slightly between adjacent levels

### Setting Bands

Use market data from multiple sources (Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Radford, recruiter intel). Target a percentile and be explicit about it:
- 50th percentile: competitive but not leading
- 65th percentile: strong for most markets
- 75th percentile: premium positioning, attracts top talent

Update bands annually. Markets move.

## Equity Considerations

- **Refresh grants**: Don't let equity become a retention trap. Annual refresh grants keep long-tenured employees competitive.
- **Equity education**: Most engineers don't understand their equity. Take time to explain vesting, dilution, and tax implications.
- **Transparency**: Consider publishing equity bands alongside salary bands. Opacity breeds distrust.

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **Very early startups (<10 people)**: Formal leveling adds overhead that isn't justified. Use simple titles and negotiate individually.
- **As a ceiling**: Levels should never be used to tell someone "you can't do that, it's above your level." If someone's doing the work, recognize it.
- **Without market calibration**: A leveling framework not tied to market data is just titles.

## In Reality

Pay inequity exists on almost every team. People hired in hot markets earn more than equivalent colleagues hired earlier. The fix: regular compensation reviews (at least annually) that adjust for internal equity, not just performance.

The conversation you most dread -- "I have another offer" -- should never be the first time you learn someone feels underpaid. If your 1-on-1s are working, you'll know before the recruiter calls.
