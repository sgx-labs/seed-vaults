# Diversity and Inclusion in Hiring — Actionable, Not Performative

## The Problem with "D&I Initiatives"

Most diversity efforts fail because they're performative. A training session, a statement, a diverse stock photo on the careers page. Diversity in engineering requires structural changes to how you source, evaluate, and select candidates. This note covers concrete actions, not aspirations.

## Structural Changes That Work

### 1. Fix the Pipeline (Where Candidates Come From)

The default pipeline -- employee referrals and LinkedIn recruiting -- reproduces your existing team's demographics. Expand deliberately:

- **Source from non-traditional channels**: HBCUs, coding bootcamps, community organizations, affinity groups (Women Who Code, /dev/color, Out in Tech, Techqueria)
- **Remove unnecessary requirements**: "CS degree from a top university" eliminates talented people who took different paths. Focus on demonstrated ability.
- **Rethink "years of experience"**: A bootcamp grad with 2 years of intense project work may outperform a CS grad who coasted for 5 years. Evaluate skill, not tenure.
- **Write inclusive job descriptions**: Research shows gendered language ("rockstar," "ninja," "aggressive") discourages applications from underrepresented groups. Use tools like Textio or Gender Decoder to audit your postings.

### 2. Fix the Evaluation (How You Assess Candidates)

Bias creeps in during evaluation. Counter it structurally:

- **Structured interviews**: Same questions, same order, same rubric for every candidate. Unstructured interviews are where "culture fit" becomes "like me" bias.
- **Blind resume review**: Remove names, schools, and addresses from initial screening. Several studies show identical resumes get different callback rates based on names.
- **Work-sample tests over whiteboard coding**: Whiteboard coding favors people who've practiced competitive programming. Work samples evaluate actual job skills.
- **Diverse interview panels**: If every interviewer looks the same, candidates notice. It signals who belongs. Ensure at least one interviewer from an underrepresented group on every panel.
- **Calibrate interviewers**: Track pass rates by interviewer. If one interviewer passes 80% of candidates from one demographic and 30% from another, that's a conversation.

### 3. Fix the Close (How You Make Offers)

- **Standardized offers**: Same level, same comp for equivalent candidates. Don't negotiate differently based on who pushes back.
- **Transparent pay bands**: Published salary ranges reduce the gender and racial pay gap. People who don't negotiate (disproportionately women and minorities) aren't penalized.
- **Inclusive benefits**: Parental leave (not just maternity), mental health coverage, flexible work, visa sponsorship -- these signal who you're willing to invest in.

## What to Measure

You can't improve what you don't track:
- **Pipeline demographics**: What % of applicants at each stage are from underrepresented groups?
- **Pass-through rates**: Do different groups advance at the same rate? If not, where's the drop-off?
- **Offer acceptance rates**: Are you losing diverse candidates at the offer stage? (Usually a compensation or culture signal.)
- **Retention by demographic**: Hiring is meaningless if people leave within a year.

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **As a checkbox exercise**: If leadership wants "diversity metrics" but won't fund structural changes, name that honestly. Metrics without action is surveillance, not progress.
- **To tokenize**: Hiring one person from an underrepresented group into an unwelcoming environment harms that person. Fix the environment first.
- **To lower the bar**: Diversity doesn't mean hiring underqualified people. It means removing barriers that exclude qualified people.

## In Reality

This work is slow and uncomfortable. You'll face resistance: "We should just hire the best person regardless of background." The answer: "We agree. We're removing the structural barriers that prevent us from finding the best person." Standardized processes and expanded pipelines are simply better hiring, period.

The team that reflects a diversity of backgrounds produces better solutions. This isn't ideology -- it's borne out by decades of research on team cognitive diversity and problem-solving.
