# Dealing with Underperformers — The Compassionate Approach

## Start With Curiosity, Not Judgment

When someone's underperforming, your first job is to understand why. The causes are wildly different, and each requires a different response:

### Environmental Causes (Fix the System)
- **Wrong role fit**: A brilliant backend engineer drowning in frontend work
- **Unclear expectations**: They think they're meeting the bar because nobody told them where the bar is
- **Bad management (yours)**: Insufficient direction, context, or support
- **Organizational dysfunction**: Too many meetings, no focus time, shifting priorities
- **Team dynamics**: Conflict with a colleague, feeling excluded, psychological unsafety

### Personal Causes (Support the Person)
- **Burnout**: They were a high performer who's running on empty
- **Life circumstances**: Health issues, family crisis, mental health struggles
- **Imposter syndrome**: They have the skills but the confidence is shot
- **Disengagement**: They've lost connection to the work's purpose

### Skill Causes (Develop or Redirect)
- **Promoted too quickly**: The level expectations exceed their current capabilities
- **Skill gap**: They lack a specific technical or soft skill needed for their role
- **Plateaued**: They've been at the same level too long and lost growth momentum

## The Compassionate Conversation

Do not open with "your performance isn't meeting expectations." Open with genuine inquiry.

**Start**: "I've noticed [specific observable behavior -- not interpretation]. I want to understand what's going on."

**Examples**:
- "I've noticed your PRs have been getting returned with more review comments than usual over the past month. Is something making the work harder right now?"
- "You've been quieter in design discussions lately. That's unusual for you. What's going on?"
- "The last two projects came in significantly over estimate. I want to help figure out what's blocking you."

**Then listen.** Really listen. Not "listen while formulating your response" -- listen to understand.

## The Support Framework

Once you understand the cause, match your response:

### For Environmental Causes
- Change their project assignment
- Clarify expectations explicitly (in writing)
- Fix your management (more check-ins, clearer direction)
- Shield them from organizational chaos

### For Personal Causes
- Express genuine care: "What can I do to support you right now?"
- Offer flexibility: reduced scope, adjusted deadlines, time off
- Connect them with resources: EAP, mental health support, leave policies
- Protect their reputation: don't discuss their situation with peers

### For Skill Causes
- Create a development plan with specific, achievable milestones
- Pair them with a mentor or coach
- Adjust expectations temporarily while they build the skill
- Consider whether they're in the right role (sometimes the compassionate move is helping them find better-fitting work)

## When Compassion Meets Accountability

Compassion doesn't mean infinite patience. If you've:
1. Named the gap clearly and specifically
2. Understood the root cause
3. Provided appropriate support
4. Given reasonable time for improvement
5. And performance hasn't improved...

Then it's time for a formal conversation. See `hub/pip-and-separation.md`.

The key: the person should never be surprised. If the PIP feels sudden to them, you haven't done steps 1-4 well enough.

## When NOT to Use This Approach

- **Gross misconduct**: Harassment, fraud, safety violations get escalated to HR immediately, not managed with compassion coaching.
- **When you're the problem**: If multiple people on your team are underperforming, the common factor is you or the environment, not them.
- **When it's not actually underperformance**: Check your calibration. Are you comparing them to your top performer? To an idealized version of the role? To yourself? Median performance is, by definition, normal.

## In Reality

The conversation you need to have with an underperformer is the one you've been avoiding for weeks. Every day you wait, the situation gets harder for both of you. They might be struggling and wishing someone would notice. They might be oblivious and digging a deeper hole. Either way, earlier is better.

The most compassionate thing you can do is be honest. Vague hints ("maybe consider working on X") aren't kindness -- they're cowardice disguised as tact.
