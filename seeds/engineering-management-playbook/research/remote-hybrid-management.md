# Remote and Hybrid Team Management

## The Core Challenge

Remote work isn't "office work done from home." It's a fundamentally different operating model. The teams that struggle with remote work are the ones trying to replicate the office on Zoom. The ones that thrive design for async-first communication with intentional sync moments.

## The Async-First Principle

Default to writing. Sync only when writing fails.

**This means:**
- Decisions documented in shared docs, not made in meetings
- Status updates written, not presented
- Design reviews as async doc comments first, meeting for unresolved questions only
- Code review as the primary feedback mechanism, not hallway conversations

**Why this works**: Writing forces clarity. It includes people in different timezones. It creates a record. It respects focus time.

**Why it's hard**: It's slower for the writer. It requires better writing skills. It feels "less personal." Managers used to seeing people in the office feel anxious about productivity.

## What Remote Teams Need More Of

### Over-Communication
In an office, you absorb context passively. Remotely, every piece of context must be actively shared. What feels like over-communicating to the sender is usually "just enough" for the receiver.

### Written Decisions
"We talked about it and decided..." is invisible to anyone not in the conversation. Write it down. Post it where the team can see it. Even a Slack message: "FYI -- we decided X because Y."

### Intentional Social Connection
The hallway, the coffee machine, the lunch table -- these are where trust is built in offices. Remote teams need deliberate equivalents: virtual coffee chats, team game sessions, or in-person offsites 2-4 times per year.

### Clear Working Hours
Not identical hours -- clear hours. "I'm available 10am-6pm ET, async responsive within 4 hours" is workable. "I'm online whenever" leads to always-on culture and burnout.

## Hybrid-Specific Challenges

Hybrid is harder than fully remote. The failure mode: a two-tier team where in-office people make decisions and remote people get informed later.

**Mitigations:**
- All meetings include a video link, even if some people are in a room together
- Decisions documented in writing, not made at the whiteboard
- Rotate "remote-first" weeks where everyone works from home to stay calibrated
- No "hallway decisions" -- if it affects the team, write it down

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **Fully co-located teams**: If everyone's in the office 5 days a week, optimize for in-person instead.
- **Teams that need real-time pair work constantly**: Some work (e.g., hardware debugging, physical prototyping) genuinely requires presence.
- **During a crisis**: Synchronous communication wins when speed matters. Switch to sync, resolve the crisis, return to async.

## In Reality

Some people thrive remotely. Some wither. As a manager, you need to recognize both. The engineer who does their best work alone at 6am deserves the same respect as the one who craves office energy. Accommodate both.

The biggest remote management mistake: measuring hours instead of outcomes. If someone delivers great work in 6 focused hours, that's better than 10 hours of distracted office presence. But this requires you to be clear about what "great work" looks like.
