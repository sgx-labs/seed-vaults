# Managing Up

## Overview

Managing up isn't political maneuvering. It's making sure your manager has the information they need to support you and your team. If your VP is surprised by a missed deadline, a departure, or a technical risk, that's your failure, not theirs. Your job is to make their job easier.

## The Information Your Manager Needs

### Weekly (or in your regular 1-on-1)
- **Status of key deliverables**: On track, at risk, or blocked. One sentence each.
- **People issues**: Someone struggling, flight risk, morale concern. Early signal, not late alarm.
- **Decisions you made**: "I chose X over Y because Z." Keep them informed, not surprised.
- **Help you need**: Budget, headcount, air cover, cross-team escalation.

### As They Happen
- **Bad news**: Deliver it fast, with context and a plan. "We're going to miss the deadline by two weeks. Here's why, and here's what I'm doing about it."
- **Good news**: Share wins. Not for credit -- so they can celebrate your team upward.
- **Risks**: "I'm seeing early signs of X. It's not a problem yet, but if Y happens, we'll need Z."

### Quarterly
- **Team health summary**: Engagement trends, turnover risk, growth areas
- **Strategic perspective**: Where you think the team should go and why
- **Resource needs**: Headcount, tools, training -- with business justification

## Communication Frameworks

### BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)
Executives don't read bottom-up. Lead with the conclusion.

**Bad**: "So we've been looking at the performance data for the past three weeks, and the team ran several experiments, and after analyzing the results, we think we should..."

**Good**: "We need to add a caching layer. Page load times are 3x our SLA. Here's the plan and timeline."

### The 30-Second Version
For every important message, prepare a 30-second verbal version. If you get pulled into an elevator with your VP's VP, can you explain the situation in the time it takes to ride three floors?

### The Disagree Protocol
When you disagree with your manager's direction:
1. Assume positive intent -- they have context you don't
2. Ask to understand: "Can you help me understand the reasoning behind X?"
3. Present your perspective with data: "I see it differently because [evidence]"
4. Accept the decision if they don't change their mind (disagree and commit)
5. Don't undermine the decision to your team. If you can't support it, escalate higher, don't sabotage.

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **Micromanaging managers**: If your manager wants to approve every PR, this framework won't help. That's a relationship conversation, not an information flow problem.
- **Skip-level escalation**: Don't go above your manager's head without telling them first (except for ethics or safety violations).
- **When you're the executive**: If you're the VP, the "managing up" framework inverts. See your CEO, board, or investors as the audience.

## In Reality

Your manager is juggling 5-8 teams. They cannot remember the nuances of your situation. It's your job to keep the important stuff top-of-mind for them.

The most common mistake: thinking "no news is good news." Your manager fills silence with assumptions, and those assumptions are usually worse than reality. Over-communicate, especially about risks.

When I had a project going off the rails, I waited two weeks to tell my VP because I thought I could fix it. When I finally escalated, she said: "I could have helped two weeks ago. Now our options are limited." That taught me: escalate early, when you still have options.

## Managing Your Manager's Manager

Occasionally you'll interact with your skip-level. Rules:
- Always keep your direct manager informed about these interactions
- Be concise -- their time is scarcer than your manager's
- Focus on outcomes and impact, not process details
- Don't complain about your manager. If you have issues, resolve them directly.
