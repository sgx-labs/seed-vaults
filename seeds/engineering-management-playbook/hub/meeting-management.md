# Meeting Management

## Overview

Engineers hate meetings. Usually for good reason -- most meetings are poorly run, unnecessarily long, and could have been an async message. Your job as a manager: protect your team's focus time while ensuring the meetings that DO happen are effective.

## The Meeting Decision Framework

Before scheduling, ask:

### Does this need to be a meeting?

**Yes, meet when:**
- You need real-time discussion or debate
- The topic is sensitive or emotional (feedback, conflict, bad news)
- You need quick alignment across 3+ people who have different context
- You're making a decision and need to read the room

**No, async when:**
- It's a status update (write it)
- It's FYI information (send it)
- It only needs input from 1-2 people (DM them)
- You need time to think before responding (doc + comments)

### If yes, does it need to be recurring?

Most recurring meetings outlive their usefulness. Every quarter, audit your recurring meetings: "If this didn't exist, would we create it today?" If no, kill it.

## Running Effective Meetings

### Before the Meeting
- **Agenda in the invite** -- if you can't write an agenda, you don't need the meeting
- **Required reading attached** -- don't spend meeting time reading a doc out loud
- **Right people only** -- 5-7 is ideal. Above 8, it's a broadcast, not a meeting.
- **Designated decision-maker** -- if a decision is needed, name who makes the final call

### During the Meeting
- **Start on time** -- don't punish the punctual by waiting for the late
- **First 2 minutes**: State the goal. "We're here to decide X" or "We're here to align on Y"
- **No laptops for non-note-takers** -- controversial, but effective
- **Timebox discussions** -- "We have 10 minutes on this topic. If we can't resolve it, we'll take it offline."
- **Capture decisions in real-time** -- in the shared doc, not someone's memory

### After the Meeting
- **Notes sent within 1 hour** -- decisions made, action items assigned, deadlines set
- **Action items have owners and dates** -- "Follow up on this" is not an action item

## Meeting Cadences

For a team of 6-8:

| Meeting | Frequency | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|-----------|----------|---------|
| Standup | Daily | 15 min | Blockers only, not status |
| Sprint planning | Biweekly | 60 min | Commit to sprint scope |
| Retrospective | Biweekly | 45 min | Continuous improvement |
| 1-on-1s | Weekly | 30 min | See `hub/one-on-one-framework.md` |
| Team sync | Weekly | 30 min | Cross-cutting topics, demos |
| Tech deep-dive | Biweekly | 60 min | Architecture, design reviews |

**Total meeting time per person**: ~4-5 hours/week. If it's more than 6, you have a problem.

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **Brand new teams**: New teams need MORE meetings initially to build relationships and shared context. Optimize later.
- **Crisis mode**: During incidents, meet as needed. Don't try to schedule -- just coordinate.
- **Remote-first teams**: May need slightly more synchronous time to compensate for lost hallway conversations. Offset with dedicated no-meeting days.

## In Reality

You'll create a meeting-free day (like No-Meeting Wednesday) and then schedule exceptions. Every exception erodes the norm. Protect it fiercely.

The meeting nobody wants to cancel is the one that most needs scrutiny. "We've always had this meeting" is not a reason to keep it.
