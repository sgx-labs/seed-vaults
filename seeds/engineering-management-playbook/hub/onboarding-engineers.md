# Onboarding New Engineers

## Overview

A new engineer's first 90 days determine whether they become a high performer or start looking for their next job. Most onboarding is terrible: a laptop, a wiki link, and "ask if you have questions." Great onboarding is structured, intentional, and makes someone productive AND connected within their first month.

## The 30-60-90 Day Plan

### Days 1-30: Learn and Ship Something Small

**Goal**: Understand the codebase, team, and culture. Ship one small PR.

**Week 1:**
- Dev environment set up and working (have this ready BEFORE they start)
- Meet every team member 1-on-1 (30 min each, scheduled in advance)
- Assign an onboarding buddy (not their manager -- a peer they can ask "dumb" questions)
- Walk through architecture at a whiteboard (not just docs)
- Read and discuss team charter, coding standards, on-call expectations

**Week 2-4:**
- Pair with 2-3 different engineers on real work
- Ship their first PR (carefully chosen: real work, low risk, clear scope)
- Attend all team ceremonies and get context on current sprint
- 1-on-1 with manager: "What's confusing? What's surprising? What do you need?"

**First PR matters enormously.** Don't give them a "starter task" that's actually busywork. Find a real bug or small feature that lets them touch the codebase, go through the PR process, and see their code in production. That first merge is a milestone.

### Days 31-60: Contribute Independently

**Goal**: Own a workstream. Start forming opinions.

- Take on a medium-sized project with clear requirements
- Start participating in design discussions (not just listening)
- Begin on-call shadowing (observe, don't carry the pager yet)
- 1-on-1 focus: "Where do you want to develop? What's not working?"
- Gather their fresh-eyes feedback: "What seems broken that we've normalized?"

Their "outsider perspective" is incredibly valuable at this stage. They see things the team has gone blind to. Ask for it explicitly before they assimilate.

### Days 61-90: Full Speed

**Goal**: Fully contributing team member. Owns outcomes, not just tasks.

- Own a significant feature or project end-to-end
- Join on-call rotation (with buddy backup for first two rotations)
- Contribute to retros, design reviews, and code reviews substantively
- 1-on-1 focus: "How's the role matching your expectations? What would you change about the team?"

At 90 days, have an explicit check-in: "Here's how I see your ramp-up going. You're doing well at X. I'd like to see you push on Y. How are you feeling?"

## The Onboarding Buddy

Not the manager. Not the tech lead. A peer-level engineer who:
- Has been on the team 6+ months
- Is patient and approachable
- Checks in daily during week 1, twice weekly after that
- Answers the questions people are embarrassed to ask their manager

Rotate the buddy role. It develops mentoring skills and distributes onboarding knowledge.

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **Very senior hires (Staff+)**: They need less hand-holding and more strategic context. Replace the technical onboarding with stakeholder introductions and architecture deep-dives.
- **During a crisis**: If the team is fighting fires, delay the structured onboarding by a week. Don't throw a new person into chaos.
- **Contractors on short engagements**: Streamline to the essentials: access, codebase orientation, immediate task context.

## In Reality

You'll be tempted to shorten onboarding because "we really need them productive." Resist. An extra week of structured ramp-up saves months of confusion, rework, and disengagement.

The number one complaint from new hires: "Nobody told me how things actually work here." The second: "I didn't feel like anyone cared whether I succeeded." Your 30-60-90 plan solves both.
