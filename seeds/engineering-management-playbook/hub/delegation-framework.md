# Delegation Framework

## Overview

New managers delegate too little. They think "it's faster if I do it myself." They're right in the short term and catastrophically wrong in the long term. If you're the bottleneck for every decision and every review, you can't scale, your team can't grow, and you'll burn out.

Delegation isn't about offloading work. It's about developing people and building a team that functions without you.

## The Delegation Matrix

Not everything should be delegated the same way. Use this matrix:

### Level 1: Tell (You decide, they execute)
- Security incidents, hiring final decisions, team-wide process changes
- Use when: stakes are high, they lack context, speed matters more than growth

### Level 2: Sell (You decide, explain why, they execute)
- Architecture decisions that affect multiple teams, budget allocation
- Use when: they need to understand the reasoning to execute well

### Level 3: Consult (Get their input, you decide)
- Sprint priorities, on-call scheduling, tooling choices
- Use when: they have valuable perspective but you own the outcome

### Level 4: Agree (Decide together)
- Team norms, coding standards, interview processes
- Use when: buy-in matters as much as the decision itself

### Level 5: Advise (They decide, you give input)
- Technical approach within their project, PR review standards, documentation
- Use when: they're capable and need to own it, but your input adds value

### Level 6: Inquire (They decide, they inform you)
- Day-to-day technical choices, meeting scheduling, individual learning goals
- Use when: you trust their judgment and just need awareness

### Level 7: Delegate fully (They decide, you don't need to know)
- Code formatting, individual workflow preferences, lunch plans
- Use when: it genuinely doesn't matter and your involvement would micromanage

## What to Delegate

**Always delegate:**
- Work that develops someone's skills toward their goals
- Recurring operational tasks (rotate them -- don't dump on one person)
- Decisions where you don't have unique context
- Anything someone else could do at 80% of your quality (the 20% gap is their learning opportunity)

**Never delegate:**
- Firing conversations
- Compensation discussions
- Upward escalations about your team members
- Taking blame for team failures (that's yours to own)
- Praise and recognition (delegate the work, keep the thank-yous personal)

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **During a crisis**: Collapse the delegation levels. Make decisions fast, communicate clearly, sort out ownership later.
- **With someone brand new**: Start at Level 2-3 and work up. Jumping to Level 6 with someone who doesn't have context isn't empowerment -- it's abandonment.
- **When the delegatee is overwhelmed**: Adding delegation to a full plate isn't development, it's cruelty.

## In Reality

You'll delegate something and then hover over it. You'll rewrite their doc, re-architect their design, or quietly fix their code. Stop. If you delegated it, let it be theirs. Give feedback through the process, not by taking it back.

The hardest part: watching someone do it differently than you would and being okay with it. If the outcome is good, the path doesn't matter.

When I delegated sprint planning to a tech lead for the first time, they organized it completely differently than I would have. I almost took it back. I didn't. Their approach worked better than mine. That's the point.

## The Take-Back Protocol

Sometimes you need to take delegated work back. Do it explicitly:

1. Explain why: "The scope changed and this now requires cross-team coordination that I need to handle."
2. Don't frame it as failure: "This isn't about your work -- the situation changed."
3. Discuss what they learned and what they'll own next
