# 1-on-1 Meeting Framework

## Overview

The 1-on-1 is the single most important meeting on your calendar. It's your direct report's meeting, not yours. Done well, it builds trust, surfaces problems early, accelerates career growth, and prevents surprises. Done poorly -- or skipped -- it signals that you don't care.

## The Structure That Works

30 minutes, weekly. Not biweekly. Not "when we have something to discuss." Weekly.

### Opening (5 min) — Their Agenda First

Start with: **"What's on your mind?"** Then shut up and listen.

Don't start with your agenda. Don't start with status updates. If they say "nothing," that's a signal -- either they don't trust the space yet, or they're not sure what it's for. Try: "What's been the hardest part of your week?"

### Blockers & Tactical (10 min) — Unblock Them

Ask specifically:
- "What's slowing you down right now?"
- "Is there anything you need from me or someone else to make progress?"
- "Any decisions you're waiting on?"

Your job here: remove obstacles. If you can solve it in the meeting, do it. If not, write it down and follow up within 24 hours. Nothing kills trust faster than forgotten promises.

### Career & Growth (10 min) — The Part Most Managers Skip

This is where 1-on-1s become transformative instead of just status meetings.

Rotate through these topics across weeks:
- **Skills**: "What skill do you want to be stronger at in 6 months?"
- **Projects**: "Is your current work developing you in the direction you want?"
- **Aspirations**: "Where do you see yourself in 2 years? What would need to happen?"
- **Feedback on you**: "What's one thing I could do differently as your manager?"

When I had an engineer who said "I don't know where I want to go," we spent three 1-on-1s just exploring what energized them. They realized they wanted to move toward platform work. We adjusted their project assignments, and within a year they were the platform team's tech lead.

### Action Items & Close (5 min) — Make It Stick

End every 1-on-1 with:
- A shared doc capturing 2-3 action items (who does what, by when)
- "Anything else before we wrap?"

Review last week's action items at the start of the next meeting. This creates accountability in both directions.

## The 1-on-1 Doc

Keep a running shared document. Your report owns it. Structure:

```
## [Date]
- Topics discussed: ...
- Action items:
  - [ ] [Manager] Follow up on promotion criteria by Friday
  - [ ] [Report] Draft RFC for caching redesign
- Notes for next time: ...
```

This document is gold during performance review season. You'll thank yourself.

## When NOT to Use This Framework

- **Crisis mode**: If the team is fighting a production fire, skip the career talk. Focus on "how are you holding up?" and "what do you need?"
- **Brand new reports**: The first 3-4 sessions should be almost entirely getting to know each other. Don't force structure on a relationship that hasn't formed yet.
- **Senior staff engineers**: They may prefer less structure and more strategic discussion. Adapt. Ask them what format works.

## In Reality

You'll cancel 1-on-1s when things get busy. Every manager does. The rule: never cancel two in a row with the same person. If you must reschedule, you reschedule -- you don't skip.

Some weeks will be all tactical. That's fine. But if you realize you haven't talked about career growth in a month, that's a problem.

## Red Flags in Your 1-on-1s

- Report consistently says "everything's fine" -- they don't trust the space
- You're doing all the talking -- flip it
- Action items never get done -- you're not following through
- Report starts canceling -- they've given up on the meeting
- Every session is status updates -- you're having the wrong meeting
