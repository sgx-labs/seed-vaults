# PIP and Separation Process

## Overview

Letting someone go is the hardest thing you'll do as a manager. It's also sometimes the right thing -- for the team, for the company, and often for the person. This guide covers the full arc: early intervention, formal PIP, and compassionate separation. The goal is never to "build a paper trail to fire someone." The goal is to give someone every honest chance to succeed, and if they can't, to part ways with dignity.

## Before the PIP — The Conversation Most Managers Skip

A PIP should never be a surprise. If someone first hears they're underperforming during a PIP meeting, you've failed as their manager.

**3-6 months before a PIP, you should have:**
1. Named the gap explicitly in a 1-on-1: "Your work on X didn't meet the bar. Here's specifically what I expected vs what was delivered."
2. Provided written feedback (even if informal -- an email or shared doc)
3. Offered support: pairing, mentoring, adjusted scope, training
4. Set clear expectations with a timeline: "I need to see Y improvement in Z weeks"
5. Documented these conversations in your 1-on-1 notes

If you haven't done these things, you're not ready for a PIP. Go back and do them first.

## The Formal PIP

A PIP is a structured, time-bound agreement with specific, measurable goals.

### PIP Document Contents

```
Performance Improvement Plan

Employee: [Name]
Manager: [Your name]
Date: [Start date]
Duration: [30-60 days, typically]

Current Performance Gap:
  [Specific, factual description. Not "attitude" -- observable behaviors and outcomes]

Expected Performance:
  [What "meeting expectations" looks like, with concrete examples]

Goals (measurable, with deadlines):
  1. [Specific deliverable or behavior change] — by [date]
  2. [Specific deliverable or behavior change] — by [date]
  3. [Specific deliverable or behavior change] — by [date]

Support Provided:
  - Weekly check-ins with manager
  - [Mentoring, training, reduced scope, etc.]

Outcome if Goals Are Not Met:
  [Clear statement that continued underperformance may result in separation]
```

### PIP Duration

- **30 days** for clear, measurable gaps (shipping code, meeting deadlines)
- **60 days** for behavioral or collaboration issues (harder to change quickly)
- **Never longer than 90 days** -- if someone can't improve in 90 days with support, more time won't help

## When NOT to Use a PIP

- **Gross misconduct** (harassment, fraud, safety violations) -- skip the PIP, go straight to termination with HR
- **Role elimination** -- that's a layoff, not a performance issue
- **Someone who's already checked out** -- if they've told you they want to leave, help them leave gracefully
- **When you've already decided to fire them** -- a PIP you don't believe in is dishonest. If the person has no realistic path to success, a managed exit is kinder.

## In Reality

Some companies use PIPs as a formality before termination. This is corrosive. If everyone knows PIPs are a death sentence, nobody will trust you when you say "I want to help you improve."

Use PIPs honestly or don't use them. If your company's culture has poisoned the PIP concept, advocate for changing it. In the meantime, be transparent: "This is a real chance. I want you to succeed. Here's exactly what success looks like."

## The Separation Conversation

If the PIP isn't met, or if you've reached a mutual conclusion:

1. **Do it early in the week** (never Friday -- they need access to support)
2. **Have HR present** (required at most companies, protects everyone)
3. **Be direct**: "We've talked about the performance gap, and the improvement we needed hasn't happened. Today is your last day."
4. **Don't over-explain or argue** -- the decision is made
5. **Be kind**: "I know this is hard. I want to make the transition as smooth as possible."
6. **Cover logistics**: severance, benefits, equipment return, references
7. **Keep it short** -- 15-20 minutes maximum

After they leave, address the team honestly: "Alex is no longer with the team. I can't share details, but I want you to know this wasn't sudden -- we worked on it together. If you have concerns or questions about your own standing, my door is open."

## Documentation Trail

Throughout the process, maintain:
- Written 1-on-1 notes showing feedback was given
- The formal PIP document (signed by employee and manager)
- Weekly PIP check-in notes
- Final assessment documenting whether goals were met
- Separation letter (HR typically drafts this)
