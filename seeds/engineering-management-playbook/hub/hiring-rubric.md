# Hiring Rubric

## Overview

A hiring rubric does two things: it makes your decisions consistent and it makes them defensible. Without one, interviewers pattern-match on vibes, and you end up hiring people who remind you of yourself. A rubric forces you to evaluate what actually matters.

## The Scoring System

Rate each dimension on a 4-point scale. Not 5 -- a 5-point scale lets people hide in the middle.

| Score | Meaning | Hiring signal |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| 1 | Does not meet bar | Strong no hire |
| 2 | Below bar with gaps | Lean no hire |
| 3 | Meets bar | Lean hire |
| 4 | Exceeds bar, would raise our average | Strong hire |

**Rule: A single 1 in any dimension is a no-hire.** It doesn't matter how brilliant someone is technically if they can't collaborate. It doesn't matter how pleasant they are if they can't do the job.

## Evaluation Dimensions

### Technical Competence (weighted 35%)

- Can they solve problems at the level you need?
- Do they understand tradeoffs, not just solutions?
- Can they explain their reasoning clearly?
- Do they know what they don't know?

**Signal to look for**: Not whether they get the "right answer," but how they navigate ambiguity and decompose problems.

### System Thinking (weighted 20%)

- Can they reason about systems beyond their immediate code?
- Do they consider failure modes, edge cases, scale?
- Can they make pragmatic architecture decisions?

**Signal to look for**: Do they ask clarifying questions about constraints before diving into a solution?

### Collaboration & Communication (weighted 20%)

- Can they explain technical concepts to different audiences?
- How do they handle disagreement?
- Do they listen, or just wait for their turn to talk?
- Would your team want to work with them daily?

**Signal to look for**: In pair-programming exercises, do they narrate their thinking? Do they ask for input?

### Growth Trajectory (weighted 15%)

- Are they learning and improving, or coasting?
- Can they reflect on past mistakes and what they learned?
- Do they seek feedback?

**Signal to look for**: Ask "Tell me about something you were wrong about." Strong candidates have specific, recent examples.

### Values Alignment (weighted 10%)

- Not "culture fit" (which is code for "like us"). Values alignment.
- Do they care about quality? About users? About teammates?
- How do they handle pressure and failure?

**Signal to look for**: Ask about a time they disagreed with a decision. Did they disagree constructively or just comply/quit?

## Interview Structure

For a standard engineering hire, 4-5 hours total:

1. **Recruiter screen** (30 min) -- logistics, motivation, salary expectations
2. **Hiring manager screen** (45 min) -- role fit, career goals, team dynamics
3. **Technical assessment** (60-90 min) -- coding, system design, or take-home
4. **Team interviews** (2x45 min) -- collaboration, values, technical depth
5. **Debrief** (30 min) -- structured scoring, discuss, decide

## When NOT to Use This Rubric

- **Hiring for a role very different from engineering** -- adapt the dimensions
- **When you're desperate to fill a role** -- that's exactly when you need the rubric most, but in reality, the bar drops. Acknowledge it explicitly if you choose to hire below bar.
- **For internal transfers** -- you already have signal. Use a lighter process.

## In Reality

You'll feel pressure to hire fast. Recruiters will push you to move candidates through. Hiring managers above you will ask why the role is still open.

Hold the bar. A bad hire costs 6-12 months of team productivity. An empty seat costs far less.

## Debrief Protocol

1. Each interviewer submits written scores BEFORE the debrief (prevents anchoring)
2. Go around the room: each person shares their rating and top evidence
3. Discuss disagreements -- if scores are split, dig into why
4. Hiring manager makes the final call, but if 2+ interviewers scored a 1, it's a no
