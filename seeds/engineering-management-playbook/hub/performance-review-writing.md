# Performance Review Writing Guide

## Overview

A great performance review does three things: it tells someone exactly where they stand, shows them specific evidence for that assessment, and gives them a clear path forward. Most performance reviews fail at all three. They're vague, backward-looking, and demotivating. This guide fixes that.

## The Evidence-First Method

Before writing a single word, gather evidence. Open your 1-on-1 notes, project docs, PR history, and peer feedback. For each person, collect:

- **3-5 specific accomplishments** with measurable impact
- **1-2 growth areas** with specific examples (not vibes)
- **Peer feedback themes** (what do 3+ people consistently say?)
- **Trajectory** -- are they growing, plateauing, or declining?

If you can't find specific evidence for a claim, don't make the claim.

## Writing Structure

### Impact Summary (2-3 sentences)

Lead with their biggest impact. Not a list of tasks -- the actual outcome.

**Bad**: "Alex worked on the payments migration project."
**Good**: "Alex led the payments migration that reduced checkout failures by 34% and unblocked the international expansion. They did this while mentoring two junior engineers who both shipped independently by Q3."

### Strengths (2-3, with evidence)

Each strength needs a specific example:

**Bad**: "Strong communicator."
**Good**: "When the database outage hit in March, Alex wrote the incident summary that became the template for all future postmortems. Three other teams adopted it. Their ability to synthesize complex technical situations into clear narratives is a force multiplier."

### Growth Areas (1-2, with a path forward)

Frame growth areas as the next level, not as deficiencies:

**Bad**: "Needs to improve time management."
**Good**: "Alex's next growth edge is learning to say no. In Q2, they took on the caching project while already leading payments, and both timelines slipped. The skill to develop: scoping their commitments to what they can deliver at high quality, and escalating when the load is unsustainable. I'll work with them on this in our 1-on-1s next quarter."

### Rating Justification

If your company uses ratings, justify yours explicitly:

"I'm rating Alex as Exceeds Expectations. The payments migration was a senior-level project delivered on time with measurable business impact. The mentoring of two juniors goes beyond their current level expectations. The growth area around scoping is the one thing separating them from a Staff-level rating."

## When NOT to Use This Approach

- **Very new employees (<3 months)**: Too early for a full review. Focus on onboarding feedback.
- **During a PIP**: The review process is different during performance improvement. See `hub/pip-and-separation.md`.
- **If you haven't been gathering evidence**: Don't fake it. Be honest: "I don't have enough data for a thorough review, and that's my failure as your manager. Let's set up better tracking starting now."

## In Reality

You'll write reviews for 6-10 people in a compressed window. Start early. Block 45 minutes per person minimum. Don't write them all in one sitting -- your later reviews will be measurably worse than your first ones.

Calibration sessions with peer managers help catch your blind spots. If you rate everyone "exceeds," you're not being honest. If you rate everyone "meets," you're not paying attention.

## The Conversation Matters More Than the Document

The review doc is a tool, not the destination. The conversation where you deliver it matters 10x more:

1. Let them read it first (send it 24 hours before the meeting)
2. Start with: "What stood out to you? What resonated or surprised you?"
3. Listen to their self-assessment -- it often reveals more than your review
4. End with: "What do you want to focus on next quarter?"
