# Feedback Frameworks

## SBI — Situation, Behavior, Impact

The most widely used feedback structure. Clear, specific, non-judgmental.

**Structure:**
- **Situation**: When and where
- **Behavior**: What you observed (facts, not interpretation)
- **Impact**: The effect on you, the team, or the outcome

**Positive example:**
"In yesterday's design review *(situation)*, you asked the question about database scaling that nobody else was raising *(behavior)*. That changed the direction of the discussion and we caught a major issue before implementation *(impact)*."

**Constructive example:**
"In this morning's standup *(situation)*, you gave a five-minute update on your PR *(behavior)*. It pushed the standup to 25 minutes, and two people had to leave before sharing their blockers *(impact)*."

**When to use**: Day-to-day feedback, performance reviews, real-time coaching.
**When NOT to use**: Complex situations with multiple behaviors -- it can feel formulaic.

## COIN — Context, Observation, Impact, Next Steps

Extends SBI with an action-oriented ending.

**Structure:**
- **Context**: The situation
- **Observation**: What you noticed
- **Impact**: The effect
- **Next steps**: What to do differently

**Example:**
"During the client demo *(context)*, I noticed you were reading directly from your notes instead of engaging with the audience *(observation)*. The client seemed disengaged and asked fewer questions than usual *(impact)*. For the next demo, let's rehearse together so you can present more conversationally *(next steps)*."

**When to use**: When feedback needs to lead to specific behavior change.
**When NOT to use**: Quick positive feedback -- it's heavier than needed.

## Radical Candor

Kim Scott's framework. Two axes: Care Personally and Challenge Directly.

| | Don't Challenge | Challenge Directly |
|---|---|---|
| **Care Personally** | Ruinous Empathy | Radical Candor |
| **Don't Care** | Manipulative Insincerity | Obnoxious Aggression |

- **Radical Candor** (top right): "I care about you AND I'm telling you the truth." The goal.
- **Ruinous Empathy** (top left): "I care about you so I won't tell you the hard thing." Most common manager failure.
- **Obnoxious Aggression** (bottom right): "I don't care about you but here's the truth." Brutal honesty without kindness.
- **Manipulative Insincerity** (bottom left): "I don't care and I won't say anything." Backstabbing, passive aggression.

**When to use**: As a mental model for calibrating your feedback approach.
**When NOT to use**: Don't use it as a label ("you're being ruinously empathetic") -- that's meta-feedback, not actual feedback.

## ASK — Actionable, Specific, Kind

A simpler framework for everyday feedback:

- **Actionable**: They can do something about it
- **Specific**: Not "good job" but "the way you structured the RFC made it easy to understand the tradeoffs"
- **Kind**: Delivered with genuine care for their success

## Delivery Tips

- **Positive feedback**: Public or private, be generous
- **Constructive feedback**: Always private, always 1-on-1
- **Timing**: Within 48 hours of the behavior. Late feedback loses power.
- **Balance**: Don't sandwich (positive-negative-positive). It trains people to brace for the negative after every compliment. Just be direct.
- **Ask permission**: "Can I share some feedback about the meeting?" gives them control.
